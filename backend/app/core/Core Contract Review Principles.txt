Core Contract Review Principles
Scaffolding – Labor + Rental (GC Subcontracts)
1. Rental is NOT Construction Work
This is the single most important principle.
Scaffold rental is not a permanent fixture
It is not punch-list work
It does not improve the real property
It should not be treated as “Work,” “Materials,” or “Improvements”
What we look for
GC language that lumps rental into “Work” or “Subcontract Work”
Payment, acceptance, LDs, warranty, or completion tied to project completion
Our position
Rental must be carved out or entire sections marked:
“Not applicable to rental services”

2. Payment Risk & Pay-if-Paid
GC contracts routinely shift owner credit risk to subs — this is especially dangerous for rental.
What we look for
Pay-if-paid / condition precedent language
Payment tied to Owner payment “for any reason”
Rental payment delayed until project completion or retainage release
Clauses requiring us to assume Owner insolvency risk
Our position
We do not accept Owner credit risk for rental
Rental should be paid based on:
delivery / time on rent / off-rent
not project completion
We preserve the right to:
suspend work
recover equipment
stop rental accrual only when access is provided

3. Retention (Especially in Colorado)
Retention is often inappropriate for rental.
What we look for
Automatic 10% retainage applied to rental
Retainage tied to project completion or punch list
Our position
Rental should not be subject to retention
If unavoidable in Colorado:
Maximum 5% per Colo. Rev. Stat. § 24-91-103 (when applicable)
Retention must not exceed rental risk exposure

4. Sales & Use Tax (High-Risk Area)
Clients frequently claim exemption incorrectly.
What we look for
Statements like “project is tax exempt”
Clauses shifting tax audits, penalties, or interest to us
Open-ended tax clawbacks years later
Failure to distinguish rental from materials
Our position
Rental is taxable in Colorado unless legally exempt
Exemption claims require:
valid, project-specific documentation
legal applicability to rental (often not applicable)
If exemption is disallowed:
GC must reimburse tax, interest, penalties, and admin costs
Tax treatment is based on project location, not GC assertions

5. Risk of Loss, Theft, and Damage (Rental Bailment)
This is a major financial exposure.
What we look for
Clauses making us responsible for:
theft
vandalism
damage by other trades
loss during GC control
Language extending indemnity during GC’s sole use
Our position
Once scaffold is turned over:
GC assumes responsibility during sole use and possession
We are responsible only for loss/damage caused by our negligence
Indemnity does not extend during GC’s sole use
Unauthorized modification or use by others shifts risk to GC

6. Liquidated Damages & Delay Damages
LDs are fundamentally incompatible with rental.
What we look for
LDs tied to overall project completion
“Time is of the essence” applied to rental
Delay damages or backcharges unrelated to our scope
Our position
No LDs for rental services
No damages for delay tied to substantial completion
Rental continues during GC-caused delays
Schedule obligations must reflect intermittent site presence

7. Scope, Safety, and Control
GC contracts often assign operational control without corresponding responsibility.
What we look for
Requirements for daily presence when not funded
Safety obligations without control of site or access
Requirements to attend meetings while not actively onsite
Cleanup or rubbish charges during periods we are not onsite
Our position
When not actively onsite:
GC acts as liaison
meetings require advance notice
Safety responsibility aligns with control
Cleanup only during periods of active work

8. Indemnity & Insurance
GC indemnity clauses are often overbroad.
What we look for
Broad-form indemnity
Duty to defend
Indemnity for GC/Owner negligence
Insurance requirements attempting to insure uninsurable risks
Our position
Indemnity limited to:
our negligent acts or omissions
No duty to defend
No indemnity for GC/Owner sole or concurrent negligence
Insurance must align with actual risk allocation

9. Flow-Down & Prime Contract Incorporation
Flow-downs can quietly import extreme risk.
What we look for
Blanket incorporation of prime contract
Flow-down without prior review
Prime terms controlling rental economics
Our position
Flow-down applies only to applicable rental scope
We require the right to review the prime contract
Prime terms cannot override subcontract protections for rental

10. Liens, Bond Rights, and Payment Security
This is non-negotiable.
What we look for
Advance or unconditional lien waivers
Waiver of bond claim rights
Clauses prohibiting liens for rental equipment
Exclusive remedy clauses tied to pay-if-paid
Our position
Lien waivers must be:
conditional
tied to actual receipt of cleared funds
We do not waive:
mechanic’s lien rights
bond claim rights
Rental charges are lienable where permitted by law

11. Trust-Fund Protections
Some contracts try to eliminate statutory protections.
What we look for
Language disclaiming fiduciary duties
Clauses allowing commingling or diversion of funds
Waivers of statutory trust protections
Our position
Contract language cannot waive statutory trust-fund rights
Funds paid for our labor and rental are held in trust where applicable
Any clause conflicting with trust statutes is unacceptable

12. Termination & Demobilization
Termination clauses often underpay rental exposure.
What we look for
Termination for convenience with limited compensation
No coverage for:
standing scaffold
dismantle
freight
tax through termination
Our position
Upon termination:
rental continues through termination date
dismantle and freight are compensable
applicable sales/use tax applies
No termination language that strands equipment unpaid

13. Style of Redlines (How We Negotiate)
This is important for consistency.
Our style
Short, declarative, operational
Willing to say:
“Not applicable to rental services”
We do not over-explain
We prioritize risk clarity over “GC-friendly” language
